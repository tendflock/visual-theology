Three Approaches — How Should the Companion Work?

Click the one that feels right for how your brain works. Each has trade-offs.

A

Conversation-First with Outline Sidebar

Primary interface is chat. The companion talks to you, guides the workflow, surfaces resources, challenges your thinking — all in a conversation stream. Your growing sermon outline lives in a collapsible sidebar that updates as you work.

Pros

  • Most natural — feels like talking to a study partner
  • Easy to go off-script and come back
  • Chat history captures your thinking process

Cons

  • Chat can scroll past important things
  • Hard to see structure at a glance
  • Two things to look at (chat + outline) = split attention
B

Focused Card Flow — One Thing at a Time

Primary interface is the current question. One card fills the screen: the question, the relevant resource (Bible text, commentary excerpt, interlinear data), and space for your response. When you're done, the next card appears. Chat is available via a slide-out drawer when you want to discuss. Your outline builds in the background from your responses.

Pros

  • Maximum focus — literally one thing on screen
  • Built for ADHD: no visible mountain of steps
  • Timer is naturally scoped to each card
  • Outline assembles itself from your work

Cons

  • Less fluid than conversation — more structured
  • May feel constraining when you want to explore
  • Harder to jump back and revise earlier thinking
C

Hybrid — Card Focus with Conversation Mode

Starts as focused cards, but you can "open up" into conversation anytime. The default view is the current question/task (like B). But when you need to dig deeper — discuss an interpretation, chase a cross-reference, ask "what did Hodge say?" — you tap into conversation mode and the companion engages as a study partner. When you're ready, you snap back to the next card. Think of it like a book with footnotes you can expand.

Pros

  • Focus when you need it, freedom when you want it
  • Best of both worlds: structure + exploration
  • Companion can pull you BACK from rabbit holes ("Good insight — let's capture that and move on. 12 minutes left.")
  • Natural transition between guided and freeform

Cons

  • Most complex to build
  • Mode switching needs to feel seamless or it's jarring
  • Risk of "conversation mode" becoming the default (defeating the purpose)

My Recommendation: C (Hybrid)

Your ADHD needs the focus of B, but your intellect and preaching depth need the freedom of A. The key design challenge is making conversation mode feel like a deepening of the current step rather than an escape from it — and giving the companion authority to say "time to move on" when the timer runs low.

The "cons" of C (complexity, mode-switching) are engineering problems we can solve. The "cons" of A (split attention) and B (feels constraining) are fundamental UX problems that fight your brain.
