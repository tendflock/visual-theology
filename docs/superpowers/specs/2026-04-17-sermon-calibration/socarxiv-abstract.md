# SocArXiv Preprint Submission Materials

## Title

**Open Empirical Homiletics: An Open Invitation to Build the First Distributed Research Collaborative on Sermon Effectiveness**

## Authors

Bryan Schneider (independent researcher)

## Abstract (250 words)

"What makes a sermon great?" is one of the oldest questions in Christian practice and one of the least empirically studied. Historical homiletics — Aristotle, Quintilian, Augustine, Chrysostom, the Puritans, Haddon Robinson, Bryan Chapell, Joel Beeke — offers a rich library of asserted but untested claims about what produces effective preaching. Modern empirical work in communication research, computational rhetoric, and narrative persuasion (Danescu-Niculescu-Mizil et al. 2012; Gennaro & Ash 2025; Green & Brock 2000; Bullock, Shulman, & Huskey 2021) has developed methodologies that could test those claims but has rarely been applied to sermons. Existing empirical homiletics (Pleizier 2010; McClure et al. 2004; Pieterse 2020; the Societas Homiletica seven-country refugee preaching study) has focused on qualitative reception research; no published methodology operationalizes sermon effectiveness quantitatively or tests historical rhetorical claims against modern reception data. This paper proposes an open, pre-registered, multi-denominational, distributed research collaborative modeled on the Psychological Science Accelerator and ManyBabies. It specifies a four-construct decomposition (rhetorical craft, immediate reception efficacy, transformative activation, historical influence), dual-panel measurement (expert craft raters and stratified lay listeners), Best-Worst Scaling rating instruments, feature operationalization rules permitting automatic, human-coded, and panel-rated features with strict limits on LLM-derived features in confirmatory analyses, multilevel statistical analysis with Holm correction and specification-curve robustness checks, PSA-style governance, named candidate collaborators, IRB and funding paths, and a concrete first study (N=20 sermons, 400 BWS raters, 5 traditions, one primary outcome) targeted at PCI Registered Reports. The design is released under CC BY 4.0 for adoption. The author does not intend to lead this work and explicitly invites adoption by a credentialed research team.
