Dear Dr. Pleizier,

I am a Reformed Presbyterian pastor in the United States writing with an unusual request. Over the past several weeks I have been developing a research design for what I believe would be a valuable empirical project: a distributed, pre-registered, multi-tradition research collaborative on sermon effectiveness, modeled on ManyBabies and the Psychological Science Accelerator. The design integrates historical homiletical claims (Robinson, Chapell, Beeke, Augustine, Chrysostom) with modern empirical methodology (Danescu-Niculescu-Mizil memorability, Green & Brock transportation, Gennaro & Ash dictionary-embedding analysis, Best-Worst Scaling panel ratings) and aims to produce a pre-registered Study 1 at PCI Registered Reports or PLOS ONE.

I am writing to you for two reasons. First, your 2010 grounded-theory dissertation remains the most methodologically serious single piece of empirical homiletics in print. Second, your presidency of Societas Homiletica places you uniquely well to evaluate whether such a collaborative has institutional ground to stand on.

I need to be honest: I cannot lead this. I preach weekly, I have ADHD, I hold no graduate degree in homiletics, and I have no institutional affiliation. My role in this project is convener, not principal investigator. I have released the full research design as an open CC BY 4.0 invitation (link below) so that someone with the time, credentials, and network to execute it can adopt it.

My question to you: would Societas Homiletica consider formally hosting an Empirical Homiletics Working Group as an umbrella for this? If yes, even tentatively, it would change the project's institutional footing overnight and permit credible co-founder recruitment. If no, I would still value 30 minutes of your time by video to hear how you would approach the problem.

The full invitation document and supporting materials are here: **[repository URL]** — preprint at **[SocArXiv DOI]**.

Thank you for reading. I understand if you are too committed to take this on, and I will not take silence as a slight.

Warmly,

Bryan Schneider
