# Synthesized Exegetical-to-Homiletical Methodology\n\n## Foundational Distinction: Exegetical vs. Expository\n\nYour self-description (\"more exegetical than expository\") is precise. Exegetical preaching foregrounds the interpretive work -- grammar, syntax, word meanings, argument flow -- in the sermon itself. The congregation sees the text being opened up. Expository preaching uses that same exegetical foundation but focuses on the broader teaching arc, often smoothing over technical details into a polished narrative. Both start from the same hermeneutical base; the difference is how much of the exegetical scaffolding remains visible in delivery. Your \"detailed outline\" approach fits the exegetical mold: the outline IS the exegesis, organized for oral delivery.\n\n---\n\n## PHASE 1: PREPARATION OF THE PREACHER (your Steps 1 + 3)\n\n**Tradition**: Edwards -- the preacher's own affections must be raised to match the truth being proclaimed. Perkins -- the minister must first be called and spiritually prepared.\n\n### Guiding Questions:\n- Have I asked the Holy Spirit to guard my easily distracted mind? (your Step 1)\n- Am I coming to this text as a worshiper, not merely a technician?\n- What are my own fears, sins, or doubts that this text might address?\n- Can I write a prayer for each phrase/sentence/paragraph of the text? (your Step 3 -- \"Digest\")\n\n**ADHD note**: Set the timer. 15 minutes prayer, then 1 hour devotional digestion. These are separate acts. Do not combine them with study.\n\n---\n\n## PHASE 2: TEXT ESTABLISHMENT (your Step 2 -- \"Translate the Text\")\n\n**Tradition**: Robinson Stage 2 (studying the passage), the grammatical-historical method of the Reformation.\n\n### Guiding Questions -- Translation & Text:\n1. What is my own working translation of this text? (Not a published one -- yours.)\n2. Are there any textual variants that affect meaning? (your Step 4 -- textual variants)\n3. Where does the pericope begin and end? What markers signal the boundaries? (discourse markers, change of scene, shift in audience, inclusio, chiasm)\n4. Were there any really odd words? (your existing question)\n5. Was there repetition? (your existing question)\n6. Were there any references to other texts (OT allusions, quotations, intertextual echoes)? (your existing question)\n\n### Specific Grammatical Questions (Robinson + your Step 4):\n7. What is the main verb? What is its tense/aspect/mood?\n8. What is the grammatical subject?\n9. What are the subordinate clauses, and what logical relationship do they express? (cause, result, purpose, condition, concession, means)\n10. Are there participles? What kind? (adverbial: temporal, causal, concessive, conditional; or adjectival/substantival)\n11. Is there figurative language? What type? (metaphor, simile, hyperbole, metonymy, synecdoche, irony) (your existing question)\n12. What is the literary type/genre of this unit? (narrative, discourse, poetry, prophecy, apocalyptic, parable, law)\n13. What grammatical constructions are being used, and are they significant? (your existing question)\n\n---\n\n## PHASE 3: OBSERVATION -- WHAT DOES IT SAY? (your Steps 4-6)\n\n**Tradition**: Robinson Stage 2, Chapell's \"observe, interrogate\" steps, Akin's Observation phase.\n\n### Journalistic Questions (applied to the text, not to life):\n14. WHO -- Who is speaking? Who is being addressed? Who else is present? What is the reader expected to know about them? (your existing question)\n15. WHAT -- What action is taking place? What command is given? What promise is made? What warning is issued?\n16. WHEN -- What temporal markers exist? What is the temporal setting? (your Step 6) Where is this in the timeline of redemptive history?\n17. WHERE -- What is the geographic setting? (your Step 6) Does the location carry theological significance? (e.g., wilderness, temple, Babylon, Jerusalem)\n18. WHY -- Why is this detail included at this point? (your existing question) Why did the character say or do this? (your existing question)\n19. HOW -- How does this unit relate to what comes before and after? How does the argument progress?\n\n### Cultural/Historical Questions (your Step 6):\n20. Was this expected/acceptable behavior at the time? (your existing question)\n21. What might be the original audience's visceral reaction to this? (your existing question)\n22. Are there cultural things that color this -- customs, social dynamics, economic realities, political context? (your existing question)\n23. What would a first-century (or ancient Israelite) hearer understand that a modern audience would miss?\n\n---\n\n## PHASE 4: WORD STUDY -- WHAT CARRIES THEOLOGICAL WEIGHT? (enriches your Steps 2 + 4)\n\n**Tradition**: The grammatical-historical method, Perkins' insistence on \"the natural sense of the passage.\"\n\n### Guiding Questions:\n24. Which words in this passage carry theological weight? (i.e., words that, if you changed them, the meaning of the passage would fundamentally shift)\n25. What is the semantic range of each key term? What does it mean elsewhere in this author's writings? In this Testament? In the LXX/Hebrew Bible?\n26. Is this a technical term? (e.g., dikaiosyne, hesed, berith, hilasterion, logos, sarx)\n27. Does this word appear in a word group that matters? (e.g., pistis/pisteuo/pistos)\n28. What is the etymological background -- and does it actually matter here, or is that a root fallacy?\n29. What lexicon entries (BDAG, HALOT, NIDNTTE, TDNT) illuminate the usage in context?\n30. Are there any hapax legomena or rare forms that require special attention?\n\n**ADHD note**: Do NOT study every word. Identify 2-4 words max that carry the freight. Use your interlinear data from Logos to identify candidates quickly.\n\n---\n\n## PHASE 5: CONTEXT -- CONCENTRIC CIRCLES (your Steps 6-7)\n\n**Tradition**: Robinson Stages 1-2, Strauss Steps 3-4 (big picture, thesis statement).\n\n### Immediate Context:\n31. What comes directly before this pericope? What comes directly after? Why does this matter?\n32. Are there surrounding units of thought that should be kept in mind? (your existing question)\n33. How does this unit function within the argument/narrative of the chapter?\n\n### Book-Level Context:\n34. What is the point of this passage in the larger picture of this book? (your existing question)\n35. What is the author's stated or implied purpose for writing this book?\n36. Where are we in the author's argument or narrative arc?\n\n### Canonical Context:\n37. Where does this story fit within the canonical and historical redemptive context? (your existing question)\n38. How does it fit with another part of the complete unit of thought (CUT), book, or Bible? (your existing question)\n39. Is this passage referenced or quoted elsewhere in Scripture? Does the NT give us a hermeneutical lens for understanding it? (your existing question)\n40. What parallel passages exist? (Synoptic parallels, Chronicles/Kings, Paul's repeated themes)\n\n---\n\n## PHASE 6: THEOLOGICAL ANALYSIS (your Steps 8-11)\n\n### 6A: Identify Christ -- Redemptive-Historical (your Step 8)\n\n**Tradition**: Chapell's redemptive arc, Clowney's preaching Christ from all of Scripture, Goldsworthy's biblical theology, Greidanus' roads to Christ.\n\n#### The Seven Roads to Christ (from your existing workflow, after Greidanus):\n41. **Promise-Fulfillment**: Is there a messianic hope? A promise that finds fulfillment in Christ? (your existing question)\n42. **Typology**: Is there a historical person, action, event, or institution that prefigures Christ? Is there escalation from type to antitype? (your existing questions)\n43. **Analogy**: Are there parallel situations between God's work with Israel and God's work in Christ with the church? (your existing question)\n44. **Longitudinal Themes**: What truth about God's saving work is disclosed here? How does it carry forward through revelation to find fulfillment in Christ? (your existing question) Key highways: Kingdom of God, covenant, providence, presence of God, love/grace/justice of God, redemption, law, sacrifice, concern for the poor, mediator, Day of the Lord.\n45. **Contrast**: What changes from Old Covenant to New in Christ? (your existing question)\n46. **NT Reference**: Does the NT cite or allude to this passage? (your existing question)\n\n#### Three-Level Redemptive History (your existing framework):\n47. What is the bottom level -- personal history?\n48. What is the middle level -- national/corporate history?\n49. What is the top level -- redemptive history (God's overarching plan)?\n\n#### Core Theological Questions:\n50. What does this passage reveal about God -- his character, his will, his ways?\n51. What does this passage reveal about humanity -- our need, our sin, our dignity?\n52. What does this passage reveal about the remedy -- grace, Christ, the Spirit?\n\n### 6B: Systematic & Biblical Theology (your Step 9)\n\n**Tradition**: Perkins' \"gathering profitable points of doctrine,\" Edwards' theological depth.\n\n53. Are there any systematic theology issues that need to be examined or explained? (your existing question)\n54. What doctrine does this passage teach, presuppose, or illustrate? (Perkins: what is the \"doctrine\" of this text?)\n55. Does this passage connect to any locus of systematic theology? (theology proper, Christology, pneumatology, soteriology, ecclesiology, eschatology)\n56. Are there any biblical theology issues -- themes that arc across the canon -- that need to be examined? (your existing question)\n\n### 6C: Historical Theology (your Steps 10-11)\n\n**Tradition**: Perkins (standing on the Reformation), Edwards (continuity with the Reformed tradition), York (confessional fidelity).\n\n57. Are there significant writings from the church fathers that illuminate this passage? (your existing question)\n58. Are there confessional documents that reference this passage? (your existing question) Check: Westminster Standards, Three Forms of Unity, Second Helvetic, etc.\n59. How have major interpreters (Augustine, Calvin, Owen, Edwards, Hodge, Murray, etc.) understood this text?\n60. Is there a historical controversy connected to this passage that the congregation should know about?\n\n### 6D: Commentary Consultation (your Step 12)\n\n**ADHD note from your workflow**: \"Don't get bogged down. You know the temptation to spend all week reading commentaries and waiting until Sunday morning to write your sermon. Don't do that. Just find what you need.\"\n\n61. What specific questions remain unanswered after my own exegetical work?\n62. Do the commentaries confirm or challenge my exegetical conclusions?\n63. Is there a consensus reading I'm departing from -- and if so, can I justify it?\n\n---\n\n## PHASE 7: THE BRIDGE -- FROM EXEGESIS TO SERMON (your Step 13)\n\nThis is the critical transition. Everything above is exegetical labor. Everything below is homiletical construction. Your workflow rightly identifies this as a distinct phase.\n\n### 7A: Determine the Subject (Robinson Stage 3)\n\n**Robinson's question**: What is the author talking about? The subject is the TOPIC of the passage, stated as a question or phrase.\n\n64. What question is this CUT designed to answer about God -- his nature or covenant-keeping -- to the original audience? (your existing question)\n65. What is the ONE thing this passage is about? (your existing question)\n66. What is the unifying center? (your existing question)\n67. What aspect of God's character is being pointed out? (your existing question)\n\n### 7B: Determine the Complement (Robinson Stage 3)\n\n**Robinson's question**: What is the author saying about what he is talking about? The complement ANSWERS the subject.\n\n68. What is this passage saying about the subject? (your existing question)\n69. If the subject is a question, what is the answer the text gives?\n\n### 7C: Formulate the Exegetical Point / Original Theological Message (Robinson Stage 3-4)\n\n**The EP/OTM = Subject + Complement, stated as a single propositional sentence about the original context.**\n\n70. What was God saying through his Spirit through the author to the recipients of that time? (your existing question)\n71. Is the proposed EP a simple sentence? (Keep narrowing until it is.) (your existing question)\n72. Is the EP propositional -- can it be affirmed or denied?\n73. Does the EP relate every sentence in the passage to the unifying theme? (your existing question)\n\n### 7D: Develop the Exegetical Outline (Robinson Stage 8)\n\n74. What is the internal logical flow of the text? (your existing question)\n75. What textual evidence supports the EP?\n76. Does every point in the outline support the EP? (your existing question)\n77. How does each point progress the argument? (your existing question)\n78. How does each point relate to the point before and after it? (your existing question)\n\n---\n\n## PHASE 8: THE FALLEN CONDITION FOCUS (your Step 13, sub-step \"ID the FCF\")\n\n**Tradition**: Chapell -- this is the hinge. Without the FCF, you have a lecture. With it, you have a sermon.\n\n**Your own note**: \"A lecture describes the text. A sermon moves it from 'this is what God said to them' into the realm of, 'This is God's direction for you today.'\"\n\n79. What does the text say? (Chapell Q1)\n80. What spiritual concern(s) did the text address in its original context? (Chapell Q2) (your existing question)\n81. What spiritual concerns do listeners share in common with those to/about whom the text was written? (Chapell Q3) (your existing question)\n82. What is the depravity factor? What in humanity rebels against this text's vision of God? (your existing question)\n83. What human condition necessitates the grace of this passage?\n\n---\n\n## PHASE 9: THE HOMILETICAL POINT / TAKE-HOME TRUTH (your Step 13, sub-step \"HP/THT\")\n\n**Tradition**: Robinson Stage 5 (homiletical idea), Chapell (redemptive exposition), Perkins (doctrine stated for the congregation).\n\n**Your own note**: \"A professor lectures. A pastor heralds God's word to His people today.\"\n\n84. What is God through me saying to his people today? What is the burden of the text? (your existing question)\n85. Is the HP based on the EP? (your existing question)\n86. Is it written in the 2nd person -- addressed to \"you,\" not to an anonymous \"someone\"? (your existing question)\n87. Is it sharp? Will it stick? Does it get under the skin? Will people remember the point on Wednesday? (your existing question)\n88. Does the HP have an eye toward the FCF? (your existing question)\n89. Have I taken the time-specific language of the EP and replaced it with contemporary equivalent language? (your existing question)\n90. Does the HP read Christologically? (your existing question -- \"A christless sermon can live in a synagogue but ought never among the blood-bought sheep of Jesus.\")\n\n**Chapell's FORM test for the outline**:\n- **F**aithful to the text?\n- **O**bvious from the text?\n- **R**elevant to the FCF?\n- **M**oving toward a climax?\n\n---\n\n## PHASE 10: PURPOSE STATEMENT & APPLICATION (your Step 13, sub-step \"Purpose Statement\")\n\n**Tradition**: Robinson Stage 6 (sermon purpose), Perkins' \"uses,\" Edwards' affections.\n\n### Perkins' Categories of Uses:\n- **Doctrinal/Mental**: What must the congregation understand/believe?\n- **Practical -- Instruction**: Consolation (comfort for the afflicted) and Exhortation (call to action for the willing)\n- **Practical -- Correction**: Admonition (warning for the wayward) and Reproof (rebuke for the sinful)\n\n### Application Questions (combining Robinson, Chapell, Perkins, Edwards):\n91. What should happen in the listeners as a result of hearing this sermon? (your existing question)\n92. What did the writer expect to accomplish by putting this here and telling it this way? (your existing question)\n93. What does God require of the hearers of this passage? (your existing question)\n94. Where does God require it of me? (your existing question)\n95. Why must I do what God requires? (your existing question)\n96. How can I do what God requires -- and specifically, how does the gospel ENABLE this? (your existing question, with Chapell's redemptive addition)\n\n### Edwards' Affections Test:\n97. What holy affections should this text stir? (delight in God? hatred of sin? hope in promises? gratitude for mercy? compassion for others? fear of the Lord? longing for Christ's return?)\n98. Have I raised my own affections to match the truth being proclaimed?\n\n### Application Categories (your existing framework):\n- Applications of **faith**: believe, receive, trust\n- Applications that are **cognitive**: understand, take to heart\n- Applications that are **physical**: go, flee, feed, wash\n- Applications that are **spiritual**: repent, hate, love, enjoy\n- Is there a measurable goal? (your existing question)\n\n---\n\n## PHASE 11: SERMON CONSTRUCTION (your Steps 14)\n\n**Tradition**: Robinson Stages 8-10, Chapell's outline structure, Perkins' text-doctrine-uses.\n\n### For Each Main Point, Amplify With (your existing framework):\n- **Orientation**: Where are we in the text? In the sermon? In time/geography/logic?\n- **Explanation**: Prove the point psychologically and emotionally. What wouldn't make sense to a modern audience? (Geography, customs, names, history, theology, language)\n- **Objections & Defense**: Validate the point against emotional or intellectual doubts. \"Is this true? Can I buy this?\"\n- **Illustration**: Frame a picture. Isolate the event and associate it with a concept. Illustrations should be FELT. (But: \"Slip into an illustration, shed the light, and get out.\")\n- **Application**: Build around the vision of God and the depravity factor (FCF). \"So what? What will this truth look like fleshed out in their lives?\"\n\n### Application Prods (your existing deep-dive questions):\n- **Why It Matters**: What if this truth were not real? Why do people need it?\n- **Dissecting the Message**: What are the 3 most important words and why? If you had 3 minutes to tell this truth to a stranger, what would you emphasize?\n- **Clarifying the Message**: How could someone misunderstand? Over-apply? Under-apply?\n- **Analyzing Our Reaction**: How do I respond to this? What does my response reveal about my own need?\n- **Addressing Our Struggle**: Why do we fail to live in the good of this? When do I need this truth most?\n- **Challenging Frameworks**: How does this challenge cultural conceptions? What false beliefs can this truth combat?\n- **Shaping Loyalties**: How might this strengthen faith? Alter a life goal?\n- **Envisioning Its Realization**: What would a church characterized by this truth look like?\n- **Ministering Its Truth**: How could I encourage someone with this truth? How would I minister it to the depressed, the lonely, the angry, the arrogant?\n- **In Real Life**: Three real-life situations where this truth gives stability and hope.\n- **Pursuing Growth**: What motivations does this passage present for obedience?\n- **Reaching Unbelievers**: How would I illustrate this truth to an unbeliever? What questions would it raise for someone who has never heard the gospel?\n\n### Introduction Types (your existing catalog):\n- Front Porch (warm, welcoming)\n- Challenge/White Glove (impossible situation)\n- Mystery (veil truths to unfold through sermon)\n- Examination (self-examination before God's Word)\n- Paradox (two truths in tension)\n- Occasion (holiday, crisis, sacrament)\n- Quote, Question, Short Story, Ramrod (\"Here's what's on my heart today\")\n\n**Non-negotiable**: The introduction MUST contain the Fallen Condition Focus.\n\n### Transitions (your existing framework):\n1. Tie into the HP & FCF\n2. Summarize the previous point\n3. Introduce the next point smoothly\n\n---\n\n## PHASE 12: SELF-EXAMINATION & EDITING (your Steps 15-16)\n\n**Tradition**: York (\"the art of good preaching is knowing where to draw the line -- what you leave out can be as important as what you keep in\"), Chapell's finishing questions.\n\n### Finishing Questions (your existing list):\n99. Does the intro have a Fallen Condition Focus?\n111. Have I landed the sermon -- is it tied up so people will remember what it was about?\n\n### Then (your Step 16):\n112. Pray over the sermon: clarity, conviction, that it hits the heart, teaches the mind, proclaims Christ and Him crucified, reforms hearts, convicts of sin, provokes to good works.\n113.
