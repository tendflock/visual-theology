# Daniel Interpretive Taxonomy: Library-Grounded Survey

Date: 2026-04-23
Scope: Cross-ecosystem survey of interpretive schools, axes, and positions for the book of Daniel and its reception, drawn from the local Logos library.

## Research Question

What are the real interpretive schools, approaches, and axes of disagreement that appear in the scholarship on Daniel — grounded in the actual resources in the library rather than a training-prior taxonomy?

This survey was prompted by the need to build a visual theology series for Daniel, and the recognition that a flat five-school taxonomy (preterist / historicist / futurist / idealist / redemptive-historical) proposed from general knowledge does not reflect how the sources themselves frame the material.

## Short Conclusion

There is no single "schools of Daniel interpretation" taxonomy in the literature. Instead there are **thirteen orthogonal axes of disagreement**, and a scholar's position is a vector across those axes. A "school" is a recognizable cluster of correlated positions — for example, "Reformed inaugurated amillennial" or "dispensational premillennial futurist" — not a single-axis membership.

The preterist / historicist / futurist / idealist quadrilateral originates in and lives in **Revelation scholarship**, not Daniel scholarship. Daniel commentaries use other axes — primarily dating (traditional/sixth-century vs. Maccabean/second-century), fourth-kingdom identity (Greece vs. Rome), and little-horn identity (Antiochus IV vs. future Antichrist vs. typological). Millennial taxonomy (premil / amil / postmil) lives in systematic theology. Eschatological-structure taxonomy (realized / consistent / inaugurated) lives in biblical theology.

Scholars who read Daniel and Revelation coherently tend to enforce a corresponding read: Beale's modified idealism reads Dan 7's little horn as transhistorical; Patterson's dispensational futurism reads it as future Antichrist; Koester's historical-critical reading reads it as Antiochus IV.

## The 13 Axes of Interpretation

This is the consolidated axis catalog extracted from the surveys. Each axis has named positions with direct citations from the library. These axes are the proposed data-model backbone for the visual theology project.

| # | Axis | Question it answers | Named positions |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | **Dating of Daniel** | When was the book composed? | Traditional/sixth-century; Maccabean/second-century/critical |
| B | **Fourth kingdom** (Dan 2, 7) | Which empire is the fourth? | Greece (Alexander + successors); Rome |
| C | **Little horn identity** (Dan 7) | Who or what is the little horn? | Antiochus IV; future Antichrist; Antiochus-foreshadowing-Antichrist; transhistorical evil power; papal Rome (historiographical) |
| D | **Seventy Sevens** (Dan 9:24–27) | How are the 70 sevens fulfilled? | Messianic/fulfilled-in-Christ; Maccabean-fulfilled; Symbolic/undefined; Dispensational-gap (70th week still future) |
| E | **Revelation approach** | How to read Revelation? | Preterist (partial/full); Historicist/church-historical; Futurist (historic/dispensational/modified); Idealist (radical/modified/eclectic); Eclecticism |
| F | **Millennial position** | Nature and timing of the millennium? | Premillennialism (historic/dispensational); Amillennialism (inaugurated millennialism); Postmillennialism; Chiliasm |
| G | **Rapture timing** | When does the rapture occur? | Pretribulational; Midtribulational; Posttribulational; Pre-wrath; Partial-rapture |
| H | **Eschatological structure** | How are kingdom/eschaton present vs. future? | Consistent (Schweitzer/Weiss); Realized (Dodd); Inaugurated/already-not-yet (Ladd, Hoekema, Vos, Bavinck); Restoration (Sanders) |
| I | **Kingdom ontology** | What kind of thing is the kingdom? | Reign (dynamic) vs. Realm (territorial); Political / Spiritual / Cosmic |
| J | **Son of Man reading** (Dan 7:13) | Who is the "one like a son of man"? | Circumlocution ("a human one"); Corporate (Israel/saints); Messianic office (individual Messiah); Foreign import |
| K | **Dispensational vs. Covenantal** | Hermeneutical-theological frame | Dispensationalism (classical/progressive); Covenantal/Reformed; Historic premillennialism (covenantal premil) |
| L | **Apocalyptic hermeneutic** | How should apocalyptic be read? | Historical-critical; Literary/canonical; Theological-message; Allegorical (patristic) |
| M | **Canonical placement of Daniel** | Where does Daniel sit in the canon? | Writings/Ketubim (MT order, implies wisdom-apocalyptic); Prophets (LXX/Christian order, implies predictive prophecy) |
| N | **Cross-book coherence rule** | Must Dan and Rev readings cohere? | Strict coherence; Loose coherence; No requirement |

## Key Cross-Stream Insight

A scholar's "school" is a **vector** across these axes — not a membership in a single category.

### Example vectors

**G. K. Beale (NIGTC Revelation):** {A: traditional-6c, B: Rome, C: transhistorical, D: Messianic, E: modified-idealist/eclectic, F: amillennial, G: n/a, H: inaugurated, I: reign, J: Messianic-corporate, K: covenantal, L: literary-theological, M: functional Prophets, N: strict coherence}

**Paige Patterson (NAC Revelation):** {A: traditional-6c, B: Rome, C: future Antichrist, D: dispensational-gap, E: futurist, F: premillennial, G: pretribulational, H: consistent-futurist, I: realm-dominant, J: Messianic individual, K: dispensational (in eschatology though he rejects the label), L: historical-but-futurist, M: Prophets, N: strict coherence}

**Craig Koester (Revelation and the End of All Things):** {A: Maccabean/late, B: Greek/Rome-mixed, C: Antiochus-type expanded to Roman imperial power, D: anti-gap/unified, E: historical-critical-literary, F: no strong commitment, H: more realized, J: corporate-imperial-critique, K: covenantal-leaning, L: historical-critical, M: Prophets, N: strict coherence in the opposite direction}

**S. R. Driver (CBSC):** {A: Maccabean, B: Greek, C: Antiochus IV, D: Maccabean-fulfilled, E: n/a, F: n/a, H: n/a, L: historical-critical, M: Writings-respecting, N: n/a for Daniel alone}

**J. Paul Tanner (EEC):** {A: traditional-6c, B: Rome, C: future Antichrist, D: dispensational-leaning, E: futurist, F: premillennial (implicit), K: functionally dispensational, L: grammatical-historical-futurist, M: Prophets, N: strict coherence}

Two scholars can agree on many axes and disagree decisively on one. Tanner and Patterson share axes A, B, C, E, F; they differ on the commentary's explicit handling of dispensational labels. Beale and Koester differ on dating (A) but share many hermeneutical instincts (inaugurated eschatology, rejection of the dispensational gap in Dan 9, etc.).

## Survey 1: Daniel Commentaries

Eight Daniel commentaries + one Daniel handbook + one apocalyptic-genre work surveyed directly from the Logos library.

### EEC Tanner (EEC27DA.logos4)

**Named axis:** Traditional (sixth-century) vs. Critical (Maccabean/second-century).

**Quote (article 2265):** "Historically, the Hebrew and Christian tradition has consistently attributed the authorship of the book to the prophet Daniel who wrote in the latter part of the sixth century bc… Since the rise of liberal Christianity and biblical criticism in the eighteenth century, critical scholars have consistently denied the traditional date and authorship."

**Position:** Traditional sixth-century. Intro sub-headings: "Objections to the Traditional Date" (art. 2266); "A Defense of the Traditional Date" (art. 2295).

**Daniel 7 little horn (article 2557):** Fourth kingdom = Roman Empire (near/far fulfillment); little horn = future Antichrist; critical view (Greek/Hellenistic fourth kingdom) explicitly rejected.

### NAC Miller (NAC18.logos4)

**Named axis:** "The traditional view" vs. "the Maccabean thesis."

**Quote (article 1188):** "For almost 1,800 years the traditional view went virtually unchallenged within both Judaism and Christianity. Porphyry (ca. a.d. 232–303) was an exception… In spite of its origins, the Maccabean thesis has become popular today."

**Position:** Traditional/sixth-century; explicitly names opposing view "the Maccabean thesis."

### ICC Montgomery (ICC_DA.logos4)

**Named axis:** No explicit school-label introduction; implicit critical framework. Montgomery treats early Jewish tradition as "traditional implication" (article 493) that doesn't withstand internal evidence.

**Position:** Moderate critical — the author used the Daniel name from "living Jewish folkstory"; book is a second-century product but retains "a far greater amount of historical tradition" than older criticism allowed.

### EBTC Sprinkle (EBTC27DA.logos4)

**Named axis:** "Traditional-conservative scholars" vs. "critical scholars" (art. 758). Section headings: "Case for a Maccabean Date and the Fictional Nature of Daniel" (759); "Case for the Early Date and Historicity of Daniel" (760).

**Quote (article 758):** "Probably in no book outside of the Pentateuch do traditional-conservative scholars and critical scholars differ as sharply as they do over the book of Daniel."

**Position (article 760):** "This commentary affirms the traditional-conservative view that the book of Daniel is historically accurate and contains genuine, fulfilled predictive prophecies."

### TOTC Baldwin (TOTC27DAUS.logos4)

**Named axis:** Second-century date vs. traditional/sixth-century; fourth-kingdom = Greek empire with Antiochus (critical) vs. fourth-kingdom = Rome (traditional).

**Quote (article 487):** "With the beginning of biblical criticism in the seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries the viewpoint of Porphyry was widely adopted by biblical scholars under the influence of rationalism. The book was held to be the product of the Maccabean period, and to be pseudepigraphic… Thus the four kingdoms become Babylon, Media, Persia, Greece rather than Babylon, Medo-Persia, Greece, with the fourth Rome."

**Position:** Traditional sixth-century; fourth kingdom = Rome.

### TOTC House (TOTC27DAHOUSE.logos4)

**Named axis:** "Most experts" (critical/Maccabean) vs. "the traditional view" (article 88).

**Quote (article 88):** "Most experts hold some variation of the following pattern for Daniel's authorship… They think Daniel is an example of apocalyptic literature. Daniel 1–6 probably conveys legendary stories… the book originated during the reign of Antiochus IV… A few writers have held to the traditional view that Daniel wrote the book in the late sixth century bc in Babylon."

**Position:** Traditional sixth-century, with explicit humility ("Daniel does not have to write the third-person sections for the book to be accurate"). Self-consciously irenic.

### BST Davis (BST27DA.logos4)

**Named axis:** "Dominant stream of scholars" (Maccabean, pseudonymous) vs. the position defended here (sixth-century).

**Quote (article 379):** "A dominant stream of scholars… hold that the individual stories (chs. 1/2–6) were eventually brought together, but that occurred perhaps in the third century bc (ca. 250)—and many would hold the stories to be legendary. Chapters 7–12 were not combined with these stories till ca. 165 bc."

**Daniel 7 little horn (article 381):** Davis distinguishes the little horn of chapter 8 (post-Greek, Antiochus-foreshadowing) from the little horn of chapter 7 (final, "Antichrist"). Uses Johannine "antichrists before the Antichrist" language — a mediating position.

### CBSC Driver (CAMBC27DA.logos4)

**Named axis:** Not a taxonomy — Driver simply defends the critical/Maccabean date as the only tenable position. Frames opposing view as "scholars who, upon apologetic grounds, defend" traditional authorship.

**Quote (article 365):** "It used formerly to be assumed as a matter of course that the Book of Daniel was written by Daniel himself,—and there are still scholars who, upon apologetic grounds, defend this opinion. A careful survey, however, of the facts… shews that this position is not really a tenable one… the Book of Daniel must have been written not earlier than c. 300 b.c., and in Palestine; and there are considerations which make it highly probable that it was, in fact, composed during the persecution of Antiochus Epiphanes, between b.c. 168 and 165."

**Position:** Maccabean/critical.

### Dictionary of the OT: Prophets (DICTOTPROPHIVP.logos4)

Contains both a "Daniel: Book of" article and a dedicated "Daniel: History of Interpretation" article (CH4.2 / article 1043).

**Named axes:**
- For the fourth kingdom (articles 1024-1025): **"The Fourth Empire as Greece"** vs. **"The Fourth Empire as Rome."** Notes: "Conservative scholars have favored this position [Rome] partly because it avoided the perceived problem of the Median Empire and partly because it makes the visions in Daniel 2; 7 point to Jesus' coming" (1025).
- For the Seventy Sevens (articles 1028–1030): **"Messianic Interpretations," "Maccabean Interpretations," "A Symbolic Interpretation."**
- Interpretation history periodization (article 1043): "Early Jewish Tradition; New Testament; Patristic Period; Medieval Period; Reformation Period; Historical-Critical Study."

### Symbols and Reality (Ryken) (SYMBOLSREALITY.logos4)

**Named axis:** Does NOT use the preterist/historicist/futurist/idealist taxonomy. Instead proposes "obvious interpretations" tied to familiar end-times categories.

**Quote (article 67):** "The tendency of 'prophecy lovers' and biblical scholars is to turn apocalyptic writings into a set of esoterica… I propose instead that we go for obvious interpretations and regard the details as referring to familiar biblical images of the end." His list includes "the millennium (however interpreted)" — explicitly bracketing millennial positions.

## Survey 2: Revelation Commentaries

Eight Revelation commentaries surveyed from the Logos library. This is where the classical four-school taxonomy actually lives.

### Beale, NIGTC (NIGTCREV.logos4)

**Named schools:** Preterist, Historicist, Futurist, Idealist, plus a fifth: "Eclecticism, or a Redemptive-Historical Form of Modified Idealism."

**Sub-schools:**
- Preterist (two forms): AD 70 / fall of Jerusalem, and 5th-century / fall of Rome (article 3812). Plus a third form where John was "merely mistaken" (article 3813).
- Futurist (two forms): "dispensational futurism" with 9-stage chronological sequence (article 3816), and "modified futurism" without pretribulation rapture.

**Quote (article 3811):** "There have been four main interpretative approaches to the book of Revelation. These will be summarized here and only briefly evaluated, since assessment of these views will occur at various points throughout the commentary."

**Position (article 3819):** "Eclecticism, or a Redemptive-Historical Form of Modified Idealism... no specific prophesied historical events are discerned in the book, except for the final coming of Christ to deliver and judge... The Apocalypse symbolically portrays events throughout history... The majority of the symbols in the book are transtemporal."

**Daniel application (articles 3922–3942, 4017):** Beale reads Dan 2 and Dan 7 as the structural frame of Revelation. Rev 13's beast "combines in himself the horrors there distributed among the four" Danielic beasts. On Rev 1:7's Son of Man: "The first [citation] is from Dan. 7:13, which in its OT context refers to the enthronement of the Son of man over all the nations (cf. Dan. 7:14) after God's judgment of evil empires." On Rev 11:3 (1260 days): "vv 1–6 include the entire church age—past, present, and future" — the 42 months is the whole interadventual age, not a future literal tribulation. On Rev 13:5 (42 months): "the prophecy of Israel's oppression at the hands of a fiendish divine opponent have begun at Christ's death and resurrection and are continuing to be fulfilled in the persecution of the church." Inaugurated-eschatological reading of Daniel throughout.

### Koester, Revelation and the End of All Things (RVLTNNDLLTHNGS2ED.logos4)

**Named schools:** NOT the classic four. Koester uses a three-way taxonomy:
- "Future Prediction" / "Revelation and the Future" (articles 25, 26)
- "Revelation as Timeless Truth" (article 27)
- "History, Politics, and Reform" (article 28)

Plus a named modern sub-school: "dispensational premillennialism" (article 38), "rapture, tribulation, and Armageddon" scenario (article 36).

**Quote (article 25):** "One of the most important of these questions is whether the visions in Revelation refer to future events or to timeless realities... Since at least the second century, some have understood the book to be primarily a message about the future of the world... others have insisted that Revelation contains a timeless message, with its visions showing how God relates to people of every generation. Still others have proposed an alternative to these two options, preferring to read Revelation as visions concerning events that are now in the past."

**Position:** Historical-critical, literary, first-century contextual. Koester explicitly critiques the dispensational reading of Dan 9 (article 38): "Darby's system maintains that God continued fulfilling prophecies through Dan. 9:26 and then stopped the clock for over two thousand years without fulfilling 9:27; yet, when read in context, these two verses seem clearly to speak of the same period."

**Daniel application (articles 37–38):** Koester attacks dispensationalism's misreading of Dan 9:24–27. Rev 13's beasts are addressed through a demonic-trinity framework; the beast's first-century referent is Roman imperial power. The two witnesses (Rev 11) are prophetic figures encompassing Moses, Elijah, Joshua, Zerubbabel traits (article 76), not a literal future duo.

### Morris, TNTC (TNTC87REUS.logos4)

**Named schools:** The classic four, each in scare quotes: "preterist," "historicist," "futurist," "idealist."

**Quote (article 168):** "It seems that elements from more than one of these views are required for a satisfactory understanding of Revelation. We must always begin with the situation of the church to which it was written."

**Position:** Eclectic, starting with first-century situation.

**Daniel application (article 221 on Rev 13:1):** "The description reminds us of Daniel 7:2–7, but this beast is more horrible than those of Daniel; he combines in himself the horrors there distributed among the four.... The beasts of Daniel 7 are to be understood of the various world empires and it may well be that this is in mind with John's beast. In that case he stands for a final empire in which will be concentrated the frightfulness of all its predecessors." On Antichrist: "It is accepted throughout the New Testament that in the last days there will be a special outbreak of the powers of evil... We may well see in the Empire a preliminary manifestation of the evil that will one day be realized to the full in the antichrist."

### Ian Paul, TNTC revised (TNTC87REVPAUL.logos4)

**Named schools:** The classic four with Paul's own labels:
- Idealist
- Futurist
- Church historical (= Beale's "historicist")
- Contemporary historical (or preterist)

**Sub-schools:** Premillennialism, Amillennialism, Postmillennialism, Dispensational Premillennialism as an explicit fourth.

**Quote (article 199):** "Within the history of interpretation of Revelation, there have been four major distinctive approaches.… Although these eight approaches (four to Revelation as a whole and four to the millennium) are usually presented as interpretative strategies, they are in fact interpretative conclusions... In that sense, we are making use of aspects of each of the four approaches."

**Position:** Explicit eclectic.

**Daniel application:** Paul ties the four millennial positions to Daniel intertexts: on Amillennialism's weakness — "John already has numbers to describe this period—three and a half years, forty-two months and 1,260 days—and it seems impossible to reconcile the binding of Satan with Satan and the beasts' trampling of the saints." The 42 months / 1260 days / time-times-half-time from Dan 12:7 are John's language for the inter-advent tribulation period, not a future 7-year tribulation split.

### Patterson, NAC (NAC39.logos4)

**Named schools:** "Four historical approaches... a fifth position has emerged": Preterist, Historicist, Idealist, Futurist, and "Eclecticism."

**Quote (article 607):** "Accordingly, there are four historical approaches to understanding the Revelation. In addition, a fifth position has emerged in contemporary scholarly circles."

**Sub-schools:** Within Futurist: "historic premillennialism to dispensationalism." Millennial sub-schema: pretribulationism, posttribulationism, midtribulationism, partial rapturism, prewrath rapturism (article 612).

**Position (articles 611, 612):** "This commentary is written from the premillennial perspective... not only from a premillennial perspective but also from a pretribulational view." Rejects the dispensationalist label while holding its eschatology.

**Daniel application:** The two beasts function as demonic counterparts to the Trinity, and their authority extends over "the concluding 42 months of that period" — the final future tribulation. Dan 7's fourth kingdom becomes the eschatological Roman-successor empire of the tribulation.

### ICC Charles (ICC_REV.logos4)

**Named schools:** Not the classic four. Charles uses **methods**:
- §1 Contemporary-Historical Method (article 946)
- §2 Eschatological Method (948)
- §3 Chiliastic Interpretation (949)
- §4 Philological Method (950)
- §5 Literary-Critical Method (952; three sub-hypotheses: Redactional, Sources, Fragmentary)
- §6 Traditional-Historical Method (954)
- §7 Religious-Historical Method (955)
- §8 Philosophical Method (956)

**Position:** Historical-critical / source-critical; anchors in Contemporary-Historical + Eschatological + literal Chiliasm (article 949): "the prophecy of the Millennium in chap. 20. must be taken literally, as it was by Justin Martyr, Irenaeus, Victorinus of Pettau."

### Michaels, IVPNTC (IVNTCREV.logos4)

**Named schools:** Preterist, "church-historical" (= historicist), Futurist. Does not name "idealist" as a fourth. Adds dispensationalism as a distinct sub-school.

**Quote (article 171):** "Modern historical critical scholarship has assumed it is indeed about the author's social world. This used to be called the preterist interpretation of the book... A widely held older interpretation viewed the book as a prophetic survey of the history of the world, and especially the Christian church, in the centuries between John's time and our own. This view, known as the church-historical interpretation, proved itself bankrupt... Another interpretation, the futurist, is that the book of Revelation has to do with the future of the world."

**Position:** Futurist-leaning with eclectic use of imagery; rejects literal chronological blueprint.

### Wilcock, BST (BSTUS87RE.logos4)

**Named schools:** The classic four: preterist, futurist, historicist, idealist.

**Quote (article 180):** "Opinions about its historical references are broadly of four kinds: the preterist view, that it describes in veiled language events of John's own time, and nothing more; the futurist, that it is largely a prophecy of events still to come; the historicist, that it is a chart of the whole of history from Christ's first coming to his second, and beyond; and the idealist, that between messages for the first century and prophecies of the far future it deals chiefly with principles which are always valid in Christian experience."

**Sub-schools:** Premillennialism, postmillennialism, amillennialism.

**Position:** Idealist-leaning (implicit).

## Survey 3: Biblical Theology + Dictionaries

Six biblical-theology resources surveyed.

### NDBT (NDBT.logos4)

**Articles surveyed:** "Daniel" (475-481), "Kingdom of God" (1205-1209), "Eschatology" (885-892), "Jesus Christ in NT" (1154).

**Named axes:**
- "Deterministic apocalyptists vs. open-view prophets" (article 478, via Russell, softened by Goldingay)
- "Realized / consistent / inaugurated eschatology" (article 1206)
- Son of Man "circumlocution" view vs. "Daniel-7" view (article 1154)

**Quote on eschatological structure (article 1206):** "'Realized eschatology' was suggested by C. H. Dodd… Some theologians… have suggested that 'inaugurated eschatology' is a better term… In contrast to realized eschatology is the view (sometimes termed 'consistent eschatology')… Albert Schweitzer, following Johannes Weiss, stressed the influence of apocalyptic on the futurist eschatological views of Jesus. Jesus' ethical teachings constitute an interim ethic until the imminent kingdom arrives. He dies a disappointed man, because the expected kingdom does not come."

**Daniel framing:** Divine sovereignty frame; chaps 1-6 lifestyle/diaspora; chap 7 "one like a human being" culminates Gen 1:26 Adamic image; end-of-history eschatology (12:1-3). Daniel 7 seeds Son-of-Man tradition in three-fold sayings pattern.

### EDBT (EVDICTBT.logos4)

**Articles surveyed:** "Apocalyptic" (277), "Daniel, Theology of" (582), "Kingdom of God" (1381-1388), "Antichrist" (275), "Son of Man" (1273), "Last Days" (1422).

**Named axes:**
- Five-way kingdom taxonomy (articles 1382-1388): Political / Liberal-Spiritual / Consistent (Schweitzer) / Realized (Dodd) / Already-not-yet (Stein/Ladd)
- Reign vs. Realm axis
- Son of Man taxonomy (article 1273): circumlocution / corporate (Israel) / messianic office / foreign import

**Quote on kingdom ontology (article 1387):** "Both in the Old Testament and in the New Testament the term 'kingdom' (malkut and basileia) is understood as dynamic in nature and refers primarily to the rule or reign of a king. It is seldom used in a static sense to refer to a territory."

**Quote on Son of Man (article 1273):** "The term could be a synonym for 'I' or 'a human person.' Some scholars have thought it to be a corporate term including Israel (n.b., Dan. 7:18) or the church (e.g., T. W. Manson), an office Jesus expected to receive (e.g., A. Schweitzer), or a figure imported into Judaism from a foreign source."

**Position:** Already-not-yet / inaugurated.

**Daniel framing:** "Daniel is the only OT book written completely in apocalyptic language… forms an important bridge between the Testaments." Four themes: sovereignty, human pride, kingdom victory, Messiah's coming. Acknowledges "hotly debated seventy sevens." Three-fold Son of Man (present/suffering/returning).

### DNTB (DNTB.logos4)

**Articles surveyed:** "Apocalyptic Literature" (1404-1410), "Apocalypticism" (1411).

**Named axes:**
- Collins SBL genre definition
- Historical vs. mystical apocalypse
- Hanson "dawn of apocalyptic"
- Käsemann "apocalyptic as mother of Christian theology"
- Zoroastrian origins debate
- Son-of-Man: corporate / individual / imported-figure

**Quote on apocalyptic genre (article 1405, Collins 1979 definition):** "An apocalypse is a genre of revelatory literature with a narrative framework, in which a revelation is mediated by an otherworldly being to a human recipient, disclosing a transcendent reality which is both temporal, insofar as it envisages eschatological salvation, and spatial, insofar as it involves another, supernatural world."

**Position:** Historical-critical, history-of-religions.

**Daniel framing:** Daniel + Revelation = canonical apocalypses; Dan "first book in Hebrew Bible to speak unambiguously of resurrection"; Dan 10 angel-patrons; four-kingdoms Persian parallel (Bahman Yasht); historical-type apocalypse with prophecy after the fact and Antiochus-IV reference.

### DLNTD (DLNTD.logos4)

**Articles surveyed:** "Apocalyptic/Apocalypticism" (1251), "Eschatology" (1936), "Millennium" (2923-2932), "Antichrist" (1236).

**Named axes:**
- Pre/post/amillennial (article 2931, explicitly defined)
- Three interpretive phases (allegorical 4th-18th c., disparate-traditions view, theological-message view)
- Augustine's amillennial read
- Patristic "dispensationalist understanding" of seven-thousand-year week
- Four-term distinction: apocalypse / apocalyptic / apocalypticism / apocalyptic eschatology

**Quote on millennium positions (article 2931):** "Current interpretations tend to fall into one of three groups, premillennialist, postmillennialist and amillennialist… This interpretation [amillennial] was first championed by Augustine of Hippo."

**Quote on Käsemann (article 1251):** "In 1960 E. Käsemann argued in a celebrated article… that apocalyptic is the source of all NT theology."

**Daniel framing:** Revelation "draws heavily on the imagery of Ezekiel and Daniel." Millennial debates locate in Dan 7 + Rev 20 axis. "Apocalyptic eschatology" vs. plain eschatology as distinct categories.

### Vos, Biblical Theology (BBLCLTHNTSTMNTS.logos4)

**Articles surveyed:** Kingdom teaching (239-246), Prophetic Eschatology sections.

**Named axes:**
- "Ultra-eschatologists" (Schweitzer/consistent)
- "Pre-millenarian construction"
- "Post-millennialism"
- "Church-Kingdom" view
- Abstract/concrete reign-realm

**Position:** Present-spiritual + future-catastrophic (proto-inaugurated); Reformed, anti-dispensational by implication.

**Daniel framing:** Daniel folds into prophetic eschatology and the theocratic-kingship trajectory; implicit sixth-century Daniel dating.

### Goldingay OTT Vol 2 (OTTHEO2GOLD.logos4)

**Articles surveyed:** 5.9 "Is the Vision the End?" (1778-1784), "The Millennium" (1784).

**Named axes:** Pre/post/amillennial named explicitly; "already and not yet" as derived from OT not NT coinage.

**Quote (article 1784):** "Daniel 7-12 promises a final overthrow of the sequence of earthly superpowers, to be succeeded by a superpower of heavenly origin… The New Testament vision of a millennium that follows the destruction of the superpower, during which Christ and his martyrs reign on earth, recasts that promise… Christian thinking came to claim that the church is that fifth superpower, or at least that the church age is the millennium… These are the 'amillennial' and 'postmillennial' understandings of Revelation; the 'premillennial' view is that Christ will return at the end of the church age."

**Position:** Eschatological-canonical; resists timetable reading.

**Daniel framing:** Dan 7-12 as Israel's hope-vision trajectory; warns the church not to identify itself as the fifth superpower.

## Survey 4: Systematic Eschatology + Apocalyptic Genre + Reception

Six resources surveyed.

### Beeke & Smalley, Reformed Systematic Theology Vol 4 (RFRMDSYSTH04.logos4)

**Named positions:** Premillennialism, amillennialism, postmillennialism, **inaugurated millennialism** (proposed relabel), **inaugurated eschatology**, full preterism, partial preterism, historicism, futurism, idealism, dispensationalism, **historic(al) premillennialism**, preeminence doctrine; already/not-yet.

**Sub-divisions:** Pre-trib rapture, 7-yr tribulation, Israel vs. church duality (dispensationalist scheme fully catalogued, Ch. 32 §4819).

**Position:** Inaugurated millennialism (= amillennialism relabeled); anti-dispensational; anti-premillennial.

**Quote on millennial positions (Ch33, R48.2 = article 4833):**
- Premillennialism — "Christ will return before ('pre-') establishing a temporary kingdom on the earth for the reign of the saints."
- Amillennialism — "there will be no ('a-') literal millennium on earth, for Christ has already begun his reign, and the saints reign with Christ spiritually."
- Postmillennialism — "Christ will return after ('post-') the Spirit-empowered mission of the church brings about an era of worldwide peace and righteousness."

**Quote on dispensationalism (Ch32 §4819):** "Dispensationalism is a theological system that distinguishes between Israel and the Christian church as two peoples of God for whom he has different plans."

**Daniel framing:** Dan 7 = "series of human kingdoms depicted as dangerous beasts … until the arrival of another kingdom—that of the 'Son of man'" whose "complete fulfillment … pertains to Christ's reign on earth, when his saints will reign with him" (Ch30). Dan 9:24-27 = symbolic seventy sevens; rejects 360-day year and pause theory: "It seems best to take Daniel's 'weeks' to symbolize an undefined length of time" (Ch32 §4822). Dan 12:1 tribulation read typologically.

**Explicit flag (Ch31 §4809):** Names the "futurist / historicist / preterist / idealist" quadrilateral for Revelation.

### Berkhof, Systematic Theology (STBERKHOF.logos4)

**Named positions:** Premillennialism (past/present), postmillennialism (earlier Dutch / modernist evolutionary), Antichrist, Chiliasm. Dispensational premillennialism with "two second comings," "secret rapture," "three resurrections," "three judgments" (§PT6.SEC2.CH2.A.2).

**Position:** Anti-premillennial, anti-postmillennial (implicitly amillennial via "second coming a single event").

**Quote on dispensationalism (§PT6.SEC2.CH2.A.2, article 1358):** "a new form of Premillennialism … a Premillennialism wedded to Dispensationalism … They really present a new philosophy of the history of redemption, in which Israel plays a leading role and the Church is but an interlude."

**Names classical dispensationalists:** Darby, Kelly, Trotter, Scofield, Chafer, Bullinger, the two Gaebeleins, Blackstone, Gray, Haldeman, Riley.

**Daniel framing:** Dan 7:8, 23-26 "little horn" and Dan 11:35ff as background for Paul's "man of sin"; Antichrist article §PT6.SEC2.CH1.B.4.

### OT Pseudepigrapha: A Guide (Brannan) (FBOTPSEUD.logos4)

Reference directory. Daniel-stream pseudepigrapha: *Apocalypse of Daniel* (9th c. AD Greek, Byzantine-Arab wars), *Seventh Vision of Daniel* (Armenian, 5th c.), *Danielic Pseudepigraphon* paraphrased by Papias. Apocalypse-type siblings: 1 Enoch, 4 Ezra, 2 Baruch, Sibylline Oracles.

### Dictionary of the OT: Wisdom, Poetry & Writings (DICTOTWPWIVP.logos4)

**Articles surveyed:** W.10 Wisdom and Apocalyptic; C.2 Canon.

**Named axes:** apocalypse (genre), apocalyptic eschatology, apocalypticism (Hanson/Collins trichotomy). Collins's five wisdom types (mantic + revelatory → apocalyptic). Spatial vs. temporal dualism. Two-ages scheme.

**Quote on canonical placement (§C.2):** "The inclusion of Daniel in this third part of the Hebrew canon (rather than in the Prophets) may be explained (at least in part) by its distinct genre (apocalyptic)" — and because "the second canonical division was already closed."

**Daniel framing:** Dan 2-6 = mantic wisdom; Dan 7-12 = apocalyptic visions received by the sage (§W.10.3.2). Writings-not-Prophets placement is genre-motivated.

### Baker Encyclopedia of the Bible (BKRENCBIB.logos4)

**Named positions:** Premillennialism, postmillennialism, amillennialism; pretribulationism, posttribulationism, midtribulationism; realized eschatology; theology of hope.

**Quote on rapture taxonomy (§2920):** "Some, identifying this with the 70th week of Daniel 9:24–27, believe it will be of seven years duration. … These are termed **posttribulationists**. Others, known as **pretribulationists**, believe that the Lord's second coming will be in two stages … Still others, known as **midtribulationists**, believe that the church will be present for the first half of the seven years but will be removed before the severe part of the tribulation begins."

**Daniel framing:** Dan 7:13 "principal source for the title 'Son of man,' which Jesus commonly used" (§2313). Dan 12:2 highlighted as OT's "focal point" for bodily resurrection (§2305). Dating uncertain between 6th c. and 165 BC (§2297-2300).

### Eerdmans Dictionary of the Bible (EDOTB.logos4)

**Named positions:** Apocalyptic (genre, per Collins 1979); amillennialism, premillennialism, postmillennialism, chiliasm, **dispensational premillennialism** (tied to Darby's "secret rapture").

**Quote on Collins genre definition (§790):** The Collins 1979 SBL Genres Project definition quoted verbatim.

**Quote on dispensational premillennialism (§4974 Rapture):** "J. N. Darby popularized, though evidently did not originate, the notion of a 'secret rapture' … has become a hallmark of dispensational premillennialism."

**Daniel framing:** "Dan. 7–12" is "only one example of a true apocalypse in the OT" (§790). Daniel's placement "in the collection of Writings (Ketubim) between Esther and Ezra-Nehemiah" vs LXX-derived Christian ordering after Ezekiel (§1784).
